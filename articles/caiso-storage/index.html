California has added over 10 GW of utility-scale batteries while curtailment keeps growing. Five datasets, five analytical lenses — on whether the fleet is absorbing solar surplus or optimizing for market returns.
Author

Published

May 19, 2026

California has installed more utility-scale battery storage than any other state — roughly 10 gigawatts of capacity by the end of 2024, up from near zero in 2018. Over the same period, the state also curtailed a record amount of solar and wind generation: over 3.5 million MWh in 2024 alone.

If batteries are the solution to curtailment, both numbers should not be growing in parallel. Yet they are.

This article works through five datasets to ask whether California’s battery fleet was built to absorb renewable surplus — or whether it was built to do something more profitable.

What the Fleet Was Built For

Before asking what batteries do, it helps to ask what they were designed to do. Two pieces of the EIA-860M generator registry give us that.

Duration class

Battery duration — energy capacity divided by power capacity — is a direct proxy for intended use.

Co-located vs standalone

A battery sharing a Plant ID with a solar or wind generator has a direct financial incentive to absorb that plant’s output. A standalone battery has no such pairing — it charges and discharges based purely on market price signals.

The Curtailment Problem

Not all curtailment is the same

This distinction matters enormously for battery policy.

System curtailment means the whole grid has more supply than it needs. A battery anywhere on the network can absorb this by charging. Local curtailment means a specific transmission line or substation is congested — the generator behind it must back off even if the rest of the grid could use the energy. A battery can only solve local curtailment if it sits on the same side of the constraint as the curtailed generator.

If local curtailment is a large fraction of the total, the policy prescription changes: the problem is grid topology, not insufficient battery capacity.

Do Batteries Charge When Curtailment Hits?

This is the central question. If batteries respond to curtailment, their charging should peak at the same hours as the curtailment spike. If they arbitrage, they target the price trough — which in CAISO tends to fall before the physical surplus peak, because batteries want to be fully charged by the 6 PM evening ramp.

The seasonal breakdown shows why spring matters most. Spring months (March through May) account for the majority of annual curtailment volume in CAISO — the solar fleet is at full output while space-cooling demand has not yet ramped up. The battery charging and curtailment curves are closest to aligned in spring, but the gap does not fully close.

The Arbitrage Signal

The clearest evidence for price-optimized dispatch: do batteries charge when wholesale prices are lowest and discharge when prices are highest?

What the Data Shows

Data and Methods

CAISO fuel mix: Five-minute interval generation by fuel type, 2018–2024, downloaded via CAISO OASIS API. Battery storage is reported as a net value — positive when discharging, negative when charging. Files stored as annual parquet files.1

CAISO curtailment: Daily production and curtailments data from CAISO’s public reports, covering curtailment events by fuel type and reason (System vs Local). Files span 2018–2025.

CAISO SP15 LMP: Day-ahead locational marginal prices at the SP15 pricing node (Southern California). Sampled at four representative weeks per month, March 2023 through December 2024. Downloaded via CAISO OASIS.

EIA-860M: Monthly generator-level data from EIA Form 860M, May 2024 snapshot. Battery units identified by Energy Source Code MWH. Duration calculated as Nameplate Energy Capacity (MWh) ÷ Net Summer Capacity (MW). Co-location determined by matching Plant ID between battery units and solar (SUN) or wind (WND) generators within CISO territory.

Scope: Analysis is limited to CAISO (CISO balancing authority). 2025 data is included in the curtailment charts but excluded from the fuel-mix and LMP analyses where coverage is incomplete.
