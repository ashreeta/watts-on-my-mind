A data-driven approach to showcase how regulatory and market differences between California and Texas have shaped battery storage deployment.
Author

Ashreeta Prasanna

Published

This article was inspired by an image I saw a few weeks ago1, comparing the installed capacity of battery storage in the CAISO and ERCOT markets. I was intrigued by the rapid growth of battery storage in the last few years and decided to see if I could reproduce the figure from publicly available data.

Figure 1a below uses EIA-860M2 data reported as of April 2026. The net summer capacity of battery storage was used to calculate the cumulative battery storage capacity by year. The planned capacity is shown as two separate series, one filtered to reflect capacity that is more certain to come online (regulatory approval received and/or construction started), and one with the full planned total. The figure shows that both markets had roughly 16 GW of cumulative battery storage capacity by early 2026, but the speculative pipeline in ERCOT is much larger, with the planned capacity nearly double CAISO’s capacity by 2030. One thing to note — the original figure has an ambiguous x-axis, failing to show that the large growth is expected by 2030, rather than in 2025. While ERCOT’s capacity is growing faster, the gap between the filtered and total planned capacity suggests significant uncertainty.

Figure 1b shows the same fleet in terms of nameplate energy capacity (GWh). Despite both markets reaching similar installed power capacity (~16 GW), California’s batteries average roughly 3.4 hours of storage duration compared to ~1.6 hours in Texas, meaning CAISO holds more than twice the stored energy per unit of rated power — a structural difference in how each market has deployed storage.

Design and Dispatch

Next, I processed the data in order to identify the following characteristics which in turn reveal the operational models for battery storage in the two markets:

  • Duration of battery storage
  • Co-located vs standalone capacity
  • Co-located vs standalone capacity by duration
  • Seasonal dispatch patterns and nodal price

Duration of battery storage

Duration of the battery by site was calculated for each individual generator (generator id in the data) by dividing the energy capacity by the capacity of the unit. This was then categorized into bins. Figure 2 shows the cumulative power capacity for each duration category and year by state.

In California, the median battery storage duration is 4 hours while in Texas it is around 2 hours. The most plausible reason for this difference is California’s Resource Adequacy (RA) program which requires Load-Serving Entities (utilities, Community Choice Aggregators, and retail sellers) to procure and demonstrate sufficient generating capacity to cover their customers’ peak load plus a 15% planning reserve margin. LSEs must show this capacity is contracted and deliverable. CPUC and CAISO’s requirement is that a storage resource must demonstrate 4 hours of discharge duration at rated capacity to receive full RA capacity credit. Shorter-duration storage receives proportionally reduced credit — roughly 50% credit for 2-hour storage — making the incremental cost of going from 2 to 4 hours almost always worth it for the RA market.3,4,5

Texas has no equivalent requirement. Batteries earn revenue by trading energy and the duration that maximises revenue is what gets deployed. This turns out to be around two hours, short enough to allow multiple charge-discharge cycles across the daily price swing and long enough to capture the main arbitrage window.6,7

Co-located vs standalone

Co-location was derived from the EIA-860M data using Plant ID matching: a battery is classified as co-located if its Plant ID also appears as the Plant ID of a solar or wind generator within the same grid area. This is a lower-bound estimate — projects registered under different Plant IDs at the same site will appear as standalone (co-located counts are therefore likely understated, particularly in Texas where large integrated projects sometimes use separate registrations).

California has a larger share of batteries paired with solar generators while Texas has predominantly standalone battery storage, shown in Figure 3. The most plausible reasons for this difference are incentive structures. Before the Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022), battery storage could only qualify for the federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) if it was charged exclusively from an on-site solar facility. Standalone batteries charged from the grid did not qualify. Therefore many projects were paired with solar specifically to access the 30% ITC. California’s Self-Generation Incentive Program (SGIP) had a dedicated large-scale storage budget.8 Large-scale projects that could demonstrate 75%+ charging from on-site solar qualified for the higher “paired renewable” incentive tier ($2.00/W base + $2.50/W resiliency adder = $4.50/W), versus the plain storage rate. This created an incremental financial case for co-locating solar and storage, particularly during 2019–2022 when the large-scale budget was still available.

Co-location × Duration

Figure 4 below shows the share of installed capacity by duration class within each co-location type. In CAISO, standalone batteries concentrate heavily at 3–4 hours (81%), while co-located solar batteries show a wider spread — 65% at 3–4hr, 28% at ≤2hr, and a small ≥5hr tail.

Standalone batteries skew toward longer durations because as discussed earlier, they are built primarily for Resource Adequacy and CPUC’s RA program requires 4 hours of discharge at rated capacity for full NQC credit.

Batteries co-located with solar have a wider spread. The higher ≤2hr share is likely due to many pre-IRA co-located batteries (2019–2022) built primarily to access the 30% ITC rather than to optimise for RA, with duration set by developer economics.

Seasonal dispatch patterns and nodal price

I wanted to understand the relationship between storage dispatch and curtailment to see if any conclusions could be drawn about higher storage capacity being able to reduce curtailment. However, it appears that storage is still playing catch-up to solar deployment in California, and I decided to save that for a more detailed analysis in a separate article. Here, I’ve pivoted to showing average nodal energy prices alongside charging and discharging energy by season for the two markets. The intention is primarily to identify and confirm that battery operation is optimized around wholesale market prices. A few things are confirmed by these data patterns and the information on co-location presented earlier:

  • In CAISO, co-located batteries which were installed under ITC can only charge from their paired solar/wind generation. In practice this means co-located batteries cannot participate in arbitrage the same way as standalone batteries.
  • Also, in CAISO, most battery projects get their primary source of revenue from resource adequacy contracts9 while in ERCOT they rely more heavily on energy arbitrage and ancillary services.10
  • Both markets show a strong midday charging signal driven by solar generation suppressing prices, with discharge concentrated in the evening demand peak.

Growth in Other Markets

Figure 6 uses EIA-860M data to show installed capacity as of April 2026 for markets with at least 500 MW of battery storage. Beyond CAISO and ERCOT, AZPS, SRP, MISO, NEVP, ISNE, FPL, and PNM have each crossed the 1 GW mark. A few market changes which will impact battery storage deployment:

  • ERCOT’s Real-Time Co-Optimization with Batteries (RTC+B) project launched December 2025, treating batteries as unified resources co-optimized across energy and ancillary services.11
  • CAISO’s Slice of Day (SOD) framework meanwhile will change resource adequacy (RA) obligations for load serving entities (LSEs) from a simple monthly target to a 12-month, 24-hour target.
  • CAISO’s Extended Day-Ahead Market (EDAM) launched in May 2026 and SPP Markets+ expected in October 2027.12

Data and Methods

EIA-860M: Monthly generator-level data from EIA Form 860M, April 2026 snapshot.13 Battery units are identified by Technology == "Batteries". Duration is computed as Nameplate Energy Capacity (MWh) ÷ Net Summer Capacity (MW). Co-location type is inferred by matching Plant ID between battery and solar/wind generators within the same balancing authority. Both duration and co-location are analytical constructs derived from the data; neither is a field that EIA publishes directly.

CAISO fuel mix: Five-minute interval net generation by fuel type, 2018–2025, downloaded via the gridstatus.io API14 (underlying source: CAISO OASIS).15 Battery storage is reported as a signed net value (positive = discharging, negative = charging).

CAISO SP15 LMP: Day-ahead locational marginal prices at the SP15 hub, 2024–2025, downloaded via the gridstatus.io API.

ERCOT battery dispatch: Hourly ESR charge and discharge data, December 2023 to present, downloaded via the gridstatus.io API.16

ERCOT DAM Settlement Point Prices: Annual Excel files containing hourly day-ahead settlement point prices for ERCOT hubs. HB_NORTH used as the reference hub price. 2024–2025 annual files.17
