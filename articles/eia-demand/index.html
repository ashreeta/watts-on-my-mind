Exploring EIA 930: The Shape of U.S. Electricity Demand

A decade of EIA hourly electricity demand data — regional divergence and multiple structural stories visible in the shape of when America uses power.
Author

Ashreeta Prasanna

Published

May 28, 2026

I’ve been spending some time diving into EIA 930 data,1,2 with the goal of general exploratory analysis of this large dataset. The analysis revealed several interesting data patterns, but explaining these in a reasonable and verifiable manner was the major challenge. In this article, I present my analysis in a sequential format, however, the actual workflow was largely exploratory and required a lot of trial-and-error until I was satisfied with the content. Beyond scanning and statistical approaches, I’ve also reproduced some ‘storylines’ at the end of the article that have already been previously identified in other publications or articles.

Methods

My approach was to start with scanning the data at regional scale and then drill down to balancing authorities (BAs) and BA subregion scale. Here, I only reproduce BA scale analysis, because regions are too large to tell specific stories. The first step was to understand how each BA’s energy demand has changed over time — in terms of annual electricity demand, annual peak, etc. The second step was to understand how the shape of the daily load profile across BAs has been changing over time alongside changes in annual electricity demand.

Choosing a baseline year required some care. At the regional scale, all thirteen regions have data back to 2016, so I used 2016 as the baseline and measured percentage change through 2024. At the BA scale, however, data is only available 2020 through 2024. The COVID years of 2020–2021 distort statistical results, so I pivoted to using 2022 as a baseline year for BA scale analysis. Even in that shorter two-year window, a number of clear structural trends emerge.

The following steps describe the general approach:

  1. Scan for any incomplete data and exclude years or flag. Apply weather normalization.
  2. Calculate metrics (annual electricity demand, peak demand, load factor, etc.) for each BA and BA subregion for the baseline year (2022) and the final year of data (2024). Calculate percentage difference from baseline for each metric.
  3. Inspect 8760 profiles of electricity demand for BAs that are outliers (outside one or two standard deviations) and present plausible explanations.
  4. Calculate the percentage difference in spread of weekday vs. weekend electricity demand and identify outlier BAs.
  5. Inspect differences in daily profiles through difference heatmaps and present plausible explanations.

AECI — Associated Electric Cooperative (−8.4%)
AECI is a large generation and transmission cooperative serving rural Missouri cooperatives through a portfolio historically dominated by coal-fired baseload generation. The 2022 profile reflects an anomalous period: when AECI’s coal capacity was run at maximum output, and surplus energy was bid into the SPP and MISO wholesale markets. By 2024, AECI economically dialed back its coal units to serve only the baseline needs of its member cooperatives — a market re-dispatch decision. The lone spike in January marks the 2024 winter storm, where AECI had to run every asset at capacity to protect its cooperative members from localized blackouts.

CPLW — Duke Energy Progress West (−6.7%)
The dramatic drop in late September and October 2024 is due to Hurricane Helene.3 On September 26, 2024, Helene wiped out transmission towers, submerged electrical substations under floodwaters, and snapped thousands of utility poles. Over 1 million customers in the Carolinas instantly lost power, with the brunt concentrated in the mountainous CPLW footprint. The wide, sharp V-shape tracks the multi-week timeline for tens of thousands of utility workers to physically rebuild the regional grid before load could be reconnected in November.

TPWR — City of Tacoma, Dept. of Public Utilities (−5.7%)
Tacoma Power’s load profile is dominated by residential and light commercial space and water heating in a winter-peaking Pacific Northwest climate. The flattening and 5.7% structural decline are difficult to explain and some plausable explantions are two core initiatives documented in Tacoma Power’s 2024 IRP and Conservation Potential Assessments (CPA).45 To comply with Washington State’s Clean Energy Transformation Act (CETA),6 Tacoma Power aggressively deployed high-efficiency heat pumps to replace resistive baseboard heating — the highest-demand residential component. This could have resulted in reducing baseline usage throughout the spring, summer, and autumn of 2024. Tacoma Power’s 2024 IRP highlights the deployment of time-of-use pricing and critical peak rebates targeting at least 10 MW of peak shaving — visible in the winter months where the sharp shoulder peaks of 2022 were smoothed into lower, broader plateaus in late 2024.

ERCO-WEST — ERCOT West Weather Zone (−5.2%)
The West zone of Texas covers the Permian Basin — one of the fastest-growing industrial corridors in the nation. A 5.2% decline in weather-normalized demand from this region seems counterintuitive; ERCOT planning guidelines clarify it as a product of grid congestion and co-located industrial behavior.7 Flexible Large Load Interconnections — particularly cryptocurrency mining facilities operating under price-responsive tariffs curtailed load in response to high prices during scarcity events.8

AZPS — Arizona Public Service (+9.6%)
Arizona Public Service registered roughly 9% weather-normalized energy growth, but the shape of the change is subtler and more interesting than a simple demand increase. The annual peak climbed to an all-time record of 8,210 MW, up from 7,781 MW in 2022 — but the load factor also rose 5 percentage points. This combination means the curve is flattening. Metro Phoenix attracted an influx of commercial data centers that draw near-constant power regardless of time of day. The Palo Verde Nuclear Station has provided stable baseload and rapidly scaling battery storage covers the evening ramp as solar generation drops off.

FPL — Florida Power & Light (+9.6%)
Florida Power & Light’s service territory added hundreds of thousands of new customer accounts over this period, driven by Florida’s sustained in-migration and business expansion. The full structural integration of Gulf Power — absorbed into FPL’s operations — contributed to the persistent load lift. The expanding customer base, combined with record-breaking summer temperatures, pushed air conditioning demand up nearly 10%. FPL responded by steadily ramping production across its natural gas fleet and accelerating its utility-scale solar buildout.

GCPD — Grant County PUD No. 2 (+10.7%)
Grant County PUD sits on the Columbia River, offering electricity rates near 3¢/kWh — among the lowest in the nation. Between 2022 and 2024, technology companies and AI infrastructure providers flooded the county with high-density server farms; by late 2024, Grant County PUD was forced to formally cap new data center interconnections to prevent local transmission overheating.9 The massive isolated red spike in January marks the footprint of the 2024 North American Winter Storm, which pushed regional temperatures nearly 20°F below the 10-year average and maxed out local electric heating.

DOPD — PUD No. 1 of Douglas Co. (+17.6%)
Douglas County PUD shares the same cheap hydroelectric resources and has attracted similar waves of data center investment. The baseline elevation also marks the operational scaling of their 5 MW green hydrogen electrolysis facility at Baker Flats10 — an industrial, around-the-clock load that lifts the overnight minimum persistently. The January 2024 spike is visible here as well.

PSEI — Puget Sound Energy (+20.1%)
The most visually striking anomaly in the PSEI chart is the spike at the tail end of December 2024 — the moment the $436 million Energize Eastside Transmission Project went fully online.11 For years, PSE’s 115 kV lines serving the Redmond and Bellevue tech corridors were maxed out, creating a physical bottleneck that queued gigawatts of data center and commercial load that could not yet connect to the grid. The instant the new 230 kV lines were energized in December 2024, queued loads turned on simultaneously, producing the sharp terminal spike visible here. The same January 2024 winter storm is also visible here.

ERCO-NRTH — ERCOT North Subregion (+31.0%)
The Dallas–Fort Worth corridor concentrates the densest cluster of hyperscale data center campuses in the ERCOT footprint — deployments by Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta that draw near-constant, non-weather-correlated power. This industrial baseload presses the demand curve upward across all hours rather than only during heat peaks, producing the elevated and slightly flattened 2024 profile. The January 2024 winter storm is visible as a sharp isolated peak.

ERCO-FWES — ERCOT Far West Subregion (+43.3%)
The growth here is entirely different in character: the 2024 demand floor of 6.2 GW sits above the 2022 annual peak of 5.5 GW — meaning the entire 2024 profile is structurally above where ERCO-WEST peaked just two years earlier. This floor shift reflects the explosive electrification of Permian Basin oil and gas field operations — electric submersible pumps, gas compression, and water injection systems replacing diesel — combined with the rapid build-out of bitcoin mining campuses that signed some of the largest industrial demand contracts in ERCOT history.

CISO-VEA — CAISO Valley Electric (+31.4%)
Valley Electric Association serves a sparsely populated territory on the Nevada–California border in the Eastern Mojave, including the Nevada National Security Site (NNSS) — the federal complex where the U.S. conducts nuclear security testing and research. On March 12, 2024, NNSA completed a 138 kV power transmission infrastructure upgrade at NNSS that raised the mission corridor’s available capacity to 65 MW, enabling expanded operations at the Device Assembly Facility and the U1a Complex.12 In a utility with a retail peak of only ~120 MW, a 65 MW mission load addition is a near-doubling event. The 2024 profile sits above 2022 across all hours, with the overnight and weekend floors rising proportionally more than the daytime peak — the characteristic shape of a constant, non-weather-correlated institutional load.

PJM-DOM — Dominion Energy Virginia (+9.0%)
Dominion Energy Virginia’s +9.0% weather-normalized growth sits within the normal distribution, but in absolute MW terms it represents one of the largest single-BA demand additions in PJM. Loudoun County, Virginia — “Data Center Alley” — is the world’s most concentrated cluster of hyperscale campuses. The characteristic shape is a modest upward shift across all hours from near-constant server loads.

Load shapes and spread

Two derived metrics reveal how the shape of demand is shifting, independent of total volume. The day–night spread measures how much higher weekday daytime demand is relative to overnight demand: a shrinking spread signals that midday solar is suppressing daytime load or that overnight EV charging is lifting the nighttime load. The weekday–weekend spread is intended to capture 24/7 load growth — data centers, electrified industrial processes, and continuous new loads which erase the traditional weekday–weekend contrast.

Differential heatmaps

The load shape spread metrics point to where the shape is changing most. The heatmaps below show when across the year and which hours of the day those changes are concentrated — 24 × 365 grids where each cell is the mean weather-normalized percent change (2024 minus 2022) at that hour and day of year. Three region groups are shown: New York ISO zones with the largest day–night spread compression, ISO New England Zone 3, and the CAISO subregions driving the strongest duck-curve deepening.

NY — NYIS Zones
Orange = higher demand in 2024 · Blue = lower · weather-normalized % change · X-axis: hour of day (0–23 h) · Y-axis: Jan (top) → Dec (bottom)

The defining feature across these five New York zones appears to be a sustained demand reduction, year-round, from roughly noon to 20h. This pattern is the signature of commercial peak-shaving automation: under NYISO’s installed-capacity tariff, large commercial and industrial consumers are billed for their capacity obligations based on usage during the single highest-demand hour of the year, which historically falls between noon and 6 PM on hot weekdays, so building management systems maintain permanent automated curtailments across the entire afternoon window.13 The abrupt recovery at 20h reflects the simultaneous expiration of utility demand-response program windows — typically ending at 8 PM. A residential-rate counterpart to these commercial signals emerged in late 2023, when the Long Island Power Authority approved new Time-of-Day pricing for PSEG Long Island customers — designating 3–7 PM on weekdays as the on-peak window — and began a phased migration of all eligible residential accounts through 2024–2025.14 This rollout reinforces the afternoon demand-reduction signal in the Long Island zone and contributes to the sharp load rebound visible after 19h, once the peak pricing window closes. EV charging is displaced into the overnight hours: the faint red visible in the 23h–4h band through autumn and winter reflects managed-charging rules that hold vehicles idle until off-peak pricing windows open near midnight.

NE — ISO New England Zone 3
Orange = higher demand in 2024 · Blue = lower · weather-normalized % change · X-axis: hour of day (0–23 h) · Y-axis: Jan (top) → Dec (bottom)

The ISNE-4003 heatmap has a similar evening reduction in demand year round. However time-of-use rates have been present in ths BA since 2008. The most plausible explanation for evening demand loss is from Connecticut’s industrial and commercial sectors. Connecticut’s industrial electricity consumption fell 6.8% in 2023 alone, and commercial consumption declined 3.2% over the same period.15 Compounding this, Connecticut’s 2022–2024 Conservation and Load Management Plan targeted a 409 MW reduction in annual peak demand and 4.8 billion kWh of cumulative energy savings — measures that reduce baseload uniformly.16 Behind-the-meter photovoltaics contribute the pale-blue midday signature visible in the warmer months: ISO-NE recorded 134 duck-curve days in 2025, up from 107 in 2024 and essentially zero a decade prior.17

CAL — CAISO Subregions
Orange = higher demand in 2024 · Blue = lower · weather-normalized % change · X-axis: hour of day (0–23 h) · Y-axis: Jan (top) → Dec (bottom)

Across all three CAISO subregions, the heatmaps show the widening gap between midday demand and the steep evening ramp. Expanded rooftop solar keeps midday demand pale or blue throughout the year, but as the sun sets between 4 PM and 7 PM, a deep orange gradient dominates the evening hours. CAISO identifies this as the defining feature of net load peak timing, which now occurs near sunset rather than mid-afternoon.18 The persistence of evening-hour orange from January through June — despite TOU price signals — might be due to electrification growth outpacing behavioral shifting. The higher evening demand could be attributed to evening load from EV adoption and heat pumps.

Showcasing selected storylines from the data

The first part of this article was focused on starting with the data and working toward explanations. Here, I’ve worked in the opposite direction. Each starts with a known event — a weather disaster, a policy shift, a structural change in how a sector uses power — and selects the specific entity and time window needed to make that event visible in the data. These stories require one to arrive with a hypothesis and would likely be invisible in a broad annual scan.

The Solar Duck Curve

CISO-SDGE (San Diego) and CISO-PGAE (PG&E / Bay Area), summer weekdays, 2020–2024

San Diego (CISO-SDGE) has the highest density of residential rooftop solar of any major US utility territory. Its solar story is almost entirely behind the meter — rooftop panels on homes and small commercial buildings. By 2024, roughly one in four San Diego homes had rooftop panels. That solar is granular, distributed across hundreds of thousands of individual rooftops, and its aggregate effect appears directly in the EIA-930 data as suppressed midday demand.

PG&E’s territory (CISO-PGAE) is roughly five times larger and spans a fundamentally different geography: the Bay Area has moderate rooftop penetration — decent solar resource but higher housing density and more renters than San Diego, meaning per-household adoption is lower. The Central Valley, however, hosts large utility-scale solar farms ranging from 100 to 600 MW, owned by independent power producers and connected directly to the high-voltage grid. These grid-scale installations generate electricity on the supply side rather than reducing metered demand directly. The result: San Diego’s duck is steeper, deeper, and more concentrated. PGAE’s duck exists but is diluted by a larger and more industrially diverse customer base.

The COVID Lockdown Signal

NYIS-ZONJ (New York City) vs NYIS-ZONA (Upstate NY), April 2020 vs 2021

New York City entered lockdown on March 22, 2020. The demand data makes this visible in a single comparison. Normalizing both years to the 2021 April mean — the post-reopening baseline — puts the two curves on a common scale where 1.0 represents a typical April hour in a functioning city. The overnight hours (midnight to 5 AM) in the two years are nearly identical: the residential base load did not change. But as the clock reaches the morning commute window, the curves diverge.

In 2021, NYC’s demand ramps sharply from 4 AM onward, climbing from 0.81× the April mean at its overnight low to 1.07× by 9 AM — the electricity signature of several million people leaving home, entering subway stations, and filling office buildings. That ramp never materialized in 2020. The lockdown curve barely rises after 5 AM, reaching only 0.96× by 9 AM. The gap between the two curves at business hours was 11 percentage points and held steady from late morning through evening, when even the nightlife load that normally swells the 9–10 PM hours was absent. NYC in April 2020 was running at roughly 91% of its reopened-city normal during the hours that define an urban workday.

The Upstate comparison provides the control. NYIS-ZONA covers the Albany, Syracuse, and Rochester corridor — a territory built more around manufacturing, state government, and universities than around high-density commercial real estate. Its 2020-vs-2021 business-hour gap was 6.4 percentage points: real disruption, but about half of NYC’s signal. Some of this reflects genuine regional effects — manufacturing shutdowns, university closures, construction halts. But the 4.6-point difference between NYC’s gap and Upstate’s is the specifically urban-commercial component.

Winter Storm Uri

On February 10, 2021, a polar vortex split over North America and sent Arctic air deep into Texas. By the night of February 13–14, temperatures in Dallas had fallen to −2°F (−19°C) — levels not seen in over a century. The demand for electricity surged as millions of Texans who had never insulated their homes for such cold turned on space heaters, electric blankets, and resistance strip heating. Across the ERCOT grid, peak demand climbed into record territory for a February.

The grid was not prepared on the supply side. Gas wellheads froze, cutting fuel supply to thermal plants. Wind turbines — not winterized to the standards required in colder climates — tripped offline. Nuclear units shut down due to frozen instrumentation. Within 24 hours, ERCOT had lost more than 30 gigawatts of generation capacity, nearly a third of its total fleet. Millions of customers lost power in rolling blackouts that lasted days.

The 2021 peak of 25,766 MW on February 14 stands as the highest single-day demand ever recorded in this subregion. Critically, that number understates actual demand: rolling blackouts were already in effect by the time the grid reached that level. Every other February in the five-year record fits within a band between 14,000 and 23,000 MW, with the 2022 winter also elevated by a milder cold snap. After Uri, ERCOT and the Texas legislature enacted sweeping weatherization requirements for generating units. The 2022 and subsequent Februaries returned to normal demand patterns, but the supply-side improvements — insulated gas wellheads, winterized turbines, required fuel reserves — are what prevented subsequent cold events from becoming another catastrophe.

Note on data exploration

Both the approaches to storytelling from data showcased in this article have pros and cons. The broad scan approach takes a long time and can lead to nowhere, but the benefit is that you understand the data quality, stuctuture and its quicks after a while. Selecting out specific subsets of the data to support a storyline is a quicker and more effective approach, but this is susceptible to bias and there is a risk of ignoring alternative storylines that might exist in the full dataset. Data selection is an art, and having a hypotheses or domain knowledge is extremely important when working with big data, simply because a lot of large datasets rarely to lend themselves to easy classification. I will also note that LLMs tend to suggest very complex metrics for data analysis that just dont work well with aggregated or noisy data. In this analysis I pivoted to mainly looking at outliers because other load duration curve (LDC) based metrics required much more contextual knowldge of the data and markets.

Data

Source: EIA Cleaned Hourly Electricity Demand Data. The dataset applies a cleaning algorithm to EIA-930 hourly demand reports, flagging and imputing anomalous values using a Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) technique.

Releases used: - Release 1 (release_2020_Oct): July 2, 2015 to July 1, 2020 — 54 BAs and 13 regions - Release 2 (release_2025_Jan_include_subregions): January 1, 2020 to December 31, 2024 — 119 BA subregion files

Stitching: Where both releases cover the same year, Release 1 data is used through December 31, 2019; Release 2 begins January 1, 2020. The six-month overlap (Jan–Jul 2020) is not double-counted.

Full-year filter: Years with fewer than 95% of expected hours (8,760 for standard years, 8,784 for leap years) are excluded from LDC and boundary metric calculations. This affects 2015 (partial year, data begins July 2) in some analyses.

Diurnal normalization: Seasonal mean normalization is used for diurnal profiles (not annual peak). Dividing by the mean of all hours in the relevant season preserves shape differences across years without embedding the extremity of the hottest or coldest day in the denominator.

Weather normalization: BA-scale comparisons use weather-normalized demand (log-OLS regression of monthly MWh on HDD, CDD, their squares, month fixed effects, and a year trend; the resulting coefficients are used to rescale each hour to what demand would have been under climatologically normal weather). The weather normals are computed as the 2016–2024 monthly mean HDD/CDD per entity. For regions and larger BAs — which have data from both releases back to 2015 — this is a genuine nine-year average. For BA subregions, which only appear in Release 2 (from 2020), the effective baseline is the 2020–2024 mean. The weather correction for subregions carries more uncertainty than for the larger entities.

Timestamps: EIA-930 timestamps are stored in UTC. For diurnal analyses, timestamps are converted to local prevailing time per time zone before extracting hour, month, and year.

Analysis notebook: The figures in this article were produced using eia_explore.ipynb, available in the same repository. The notebook covers the demand change distribution, BA annual demand profiles, load shape spread metrics, and differential heatmaps. The weather normalization pipeline (scripts 01–05) is in LDD/weather_norm/.
