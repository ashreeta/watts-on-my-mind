Heat Pumps for the Residential Sector: Drivers of Adoption

Part 1: The Technology — Mechanics and Performance

What is a heat pump, and why is it different from an AC?

A heat pump is a device that transfers heat using a small amount of electricity to run a compression cycle. It doesn’t create heat the way a furnace or boiler does — it utilizes heat that already exists in the outdoor air, the ground, or a body of water. That distinction allows heat pumps to provide both heating and cooling.

Mechanically, a heat pump is essentially an air conditioner that can run in reverse. Both share a common architecture: compressor, refrigerant loop, indoor and outdoor coils, and fans. However, a heat pump incorporates specific elements to enable reverse-cycle operation, high efficiency, and reliable cold-weather performance. These include a four-way reversing valve to switch refrigerant flow direction, an inverter-driven variable-speed compressor, modern lower-GWP refrigerants (such as R-32 or R-454B), and advanced defrost controls with low-temperature starting capabilities.

While heat pumps in cooling mode are not necessarily more efficient than ACs, their value comes from providing both heating and cooling and the ability to operate at partial loads. Heat pumps excel at operating efficiently in moderate climates and offer flexible form factors like single-room mini-splits. They are not designed for instant high-temperature output; rather, they provide a more consistent, steady delivery of heating or cooling.

Part 2: History of heat pump deployment

Air-source heat pumps entered the mass market in the 1950s with manufacturers like GE and Carrier introducing central ducted systems, followed by the development of “mini-split” heat pumps in Japan. The 1973 oil crisis served as a global catalyst for adoption, doubling the technology’s U.S. footprint. A critical technical turning point occurred in 1980 with the Japanese invention of the inverter-driven compressor, transitioning systems from inefficient cycling operation to the variable-speed modulation seen today.

In the early 2000s, air-source heat pumps (ASHPs) were primarily installed to provide cooling. They were standard single-stage split systems whose coefficient of performance degraded steadily as outdoor temperature dropped, hitting a balance point typically between 25–35°F, where electric resistance “strip heat” had to take over. Adoption clustered in mild climates: the U.S. Southeast, southern China, and Mediterranean Europe. In those markets, a heat pump was mostly an upgrade path from electric resistance heat.

By 2010, cold-climate engineering matured and variable-speed inverter compressors, vapor injection, and advanced refrigerants became the standard. Mitsubishi’s Hyper-Heating Inverter (H2i) line, along with comparable systems from Daikin and Fujitsu, established that air-source units could deliver useful heat at sub-zero temperatures. The launch of the Northeast Energy Efficiency Partnership (NEEP) Cold Climate ASHP Specification in 2015 provided the first rigorous, third-party benchmark for low-temperature performance. This allowed utility programs to transition away from simple rebates and toward high-performance incentives. In China, the massive “Coal-to-Electricity” campaign retrofitted millions of rural homes from coal stoves to heat pumps. This campaign provided the manufacturing volumes that lowered global hardware costs.

By the early 2020s, a convergence of high natural gas prices and aggressive policy shifted the heat pump from an option to the “incumbent” heating technology. A major milestone was reached in 2022 when heat pumps officially surpassed gas furnaces in U.S. annual unit shipments—a lead that grew to 12% by 2025. As we look toward 2030, the conversation has moved beyond mere appliance efficiency toward the heat pump’s role as a vital grid asset.

Part 3: Market Deep-Dives

The specific type of heat pump technology in a particular country or region is heavily influenced by the existing technology that is being replaced. The cost of conversion is the primary influencing factor in decision-making and is more important than lifetime cost of operation.

Japan and South Korea: The efficiency leaders

Japan’s residential heat pump penetration is nearly universal. It is anchored by the “Eco Cute,” a CO2 (R744) heat pump water heater introduced in 2001 that became the national standard. Conversion costs are minimal because cooling infrastructure is already heat-pump-based. Kerosene heaters are standalone room appliances, meaning there is no central distribution to abandon. “Upgrading” typically means replacing aging mini-split heads at a cost of $1,500–$3,000 per zone. Japan represents the lowest-friction case globally because it never built the central distribution infrastructure that other nations must now decommission.

South Korea views building electrification as a national security priority, importing over 90% of its primary energy. Zero Energy Building (ZEB) certifications and subsidies anchor the policy stack. The ondol radiant floor is the dominant existing technology. Air-to-water heat pumps can drive these floor loops directly if they were sized for low-temp operation (common in modern construction; rarer in older homes). Where the conversion works, homeowners replace only the boiler for roughly $8k–$15k. Where ondol cannot accept low-temp water, the pragmatic path involves keeping the gas boiler and adding mini-splits for cooling and shoulder-season heating.

European Union: The hydronic shift

Western and Central Europe lead in air-to-water systems, integrated into existing radiator and underfloor networks. REPowerEU — the EU’s response to dependence on Russian gas — accelerated the pivot, and France, Germany, and Italy have all moved to phase out new fossil boiler installations in some form. Norway, Sweden, and Finland hold the highest heat pump penetration rates in the world; ground-source heat pumps are the dominant technology in these countries.

Hydronic radiators are the most common in Germany, making air-to-water monobloc heat pumps the natural fit. Installation costs range from €25k–€40k, with Bundesförderung für effiziente Gebäude (BEG) programs covering 30–70% of the cost. The hidden friction is radiator sizing: low-temperature heat pump operation (45–55°C) requires larger surface areas than gas boilers (70–80°C). Many homes require radiator upgrades or weatherization first, and oil tank removal is mandatory under German regulations.

In France, the market is split between the 30% of households on electric wall panel heaters and the rest in the gas/oil hydronic segment. For those on electric wall panel heaters, the path is ductless mini-splits (€8k–€18k). The hydronic segment mirrors Germany, with air-to-water systems costing €15k–€30k, after considering the MaPrimeRénov’ subsidy structure.

District heating is common in Nordic countries, and disconnecting from district heat is rarely allowed or economic. For the non-DH segment, hydronic distribution typically already exists, and air-to-water heat pumps fit cleanly. Norway is unique; high mini-split adoption came from homes with electric baseboards simply adding ductless units (NOK 30k–80k per zone). Sweden and Finland have more existing hydronic and ground-source infrastructure, so air-to-water and ground-source remain dominant retrofit choices.

United Kingdom: The UK serves as a cautionary tale where subsidies have not yet met targets. Adoption is hampered by a poor electricity-to-gas price ratio, a thin installer pipeline, and an inefficient housing stock. Most UK radiators are sized for high-temperature combi boilers (~70°C); transitioning to a heat pump often requires new radiators, the addition of a hot water cylinder, and consumer unit upgrades. Net costs after the £7,500 BUS grant often land at £8k–£14k, compared to just £2.5k–£4k for a like-for-like boiler replacement.

China: The volume engine

China is the world’s largest heat pump market and manufacturer. While domestic sales have plateaued due to macroeconomic softness, exports continue to grow. In Southern China, “conversion” is often just a usage shift toward winter heating with existing cooling units. In Northern rural areas, Coal-to-Electricity conversions are new installations of subsidized mini-splits (RMB 8k–20k), benefiting from the fact that these homes mostly skipped the central-distribution stage of HVAC development.

United States: The ducted transformation

The U.S. is unique in its reliance on air-to-air ducted systems—a legacy of the central AC era. Cold-climate inverter performance has unlocked the Northeast and Midwest, supported by IRA tax credits (25C and HEEHRA). As of late 2024, nine states had pledged that heat pumps would account for at least 65% of residential heating-and-cooling sales by 2030.

The conversion cost story in the Northeast splits sharply by housing stock. Newer suburban homes with gas furnaces and central AC are the easiest cases with a ducted central ASHP swap costing $8k–$15k. Existing ductwork is generally reusable, though heat pumps move roughly 20–30% more airflow per BTU than gas furnaces and older systems often need duct modifications or static pressure adjustments ($1–5k). Older urban housing with oil-fired radiators is much harder; conversion to mini-split heat pumps run $15k–$25k for whole-home multi-zone systems and often require oil tank removal and electric panel upgrades ($2k–$4k).

Most homes in the Midwest are forced-air with gas furnace plus central AC sharing ductwork — the easiest case to swap with ASHP. Replacement runs $10–18k for a hybrid dual-fuel setup that keeps the gas furnace as backup. This sidesteps cold-snap reliability concerns and the high cost of full gas decommissioning.

The Southeast is the cheapest conversion market in the US because the existing system is usually a central electric strip heat package or a low-efficiency heat pump sharing ductwork with central AC. ASHP replacement runs $8–14k, ductwork is fully reusable, no fuel infrastructure to decommission, no panel upgrade typically needed, and the homeowner is already paying electric heating bills so the operational case is straightforward.

In the Mountain West and Plains, gas furnace plus evaporative cooler is the regionally distinctive setup. The furnace ductwork is reusable for a ducted ASHP swap ($10–16k), but the evaporative cooler — typically roof-mounted or window-mounted — is abandoned in place or removed ($500–1,500 for removal and roof patching).

In the Pacific Northwest, a surge in cooling demand after the 2021 heat dome transformed this market. Most retrofits are ductless mini-splits added to homes with electric baseboard heat. Single-zone installs run $4–8k, multi-zone systems $12–25k. Due to this low marginal cost of conversion, this region has been a leader on heat pump deployment per capita despite a small starting installed base.

Alaska is the highest-friction market. Oil-fired hydronic systems, remote labor, and shipping costs push conversion economics over $30k, concentrating deployment in milder coastal areas.

Part 4: The Path Forward

1. Product Development and contractor capacity

Window heat pumps have proven the concept, but currently serve only a small slice of the 50 million room AC units in the U.S. We need products that fit casement and slider windows (unlocking 8–12 million households), higher-capacity units for studio apartments (14,000–18,000 BTU), and cold-climate packaged terminal heat pumps for Northeast multifamily buildings. These segments are constrained by physical fit, not financing. Targeted challenges—similar to the $263 million NYCHA Clean Heat for All Challenge—could produce these products within 3–5 years.

Simultaneously, the 2025 A2L refrigerant transition has made contractor capacity the true deployment ceiling. Many smaller installers and contractors are dropping out rather than retooling for spark-resistant hardware and updated EPA Section 608 certifications. Workforce development—apprenticeships and training subsidies—is currently underweighted. The other side of the problem is intervention design: the highest-leverage moment for adoption is the AC failure, when a homeowner is making a 48-hour replacement decision. Midstream programs that move incentives upstream to distributors and contractors (so a heat pump is cheaper than a comparable AC at wholesale) are far more effective than downstream rebates.

2. VPP Integration

We are moving from “appliance efficiency” to “system-level value.” The U.S. grid needs 200 GW of new peak capacity by 2030. Aggregating smart heat pumps into Virtual Power Plants (VPPs) could meet 10–20% of that need at a 40–60% lower cost than new gas peakers. With federal 25C tax credits having expired at the end of 2025, grid services revenue is the most credible candidate to fill the funding gap. VPP enrollment can provide homeowners with $200–$500 in annual revenue, significantly improving the economics of a conversion. As utilities like Georgia Power build new gas plants to meet data center demand, replacing aging heat pumps with high-efficiency, VPP-ready units provides a vital hedge. Whether load forecasts are accurate or inflated, efficiency gains accrue to the customer and reduce the risk of stranded utility assets.

Summary

Heat pumps are a proven, cost-competitive technology that has outsold gas furnaces in the U.S. since 2022. Expanding the product portfolio for urban housing, building the contractor workforce, and unlocking the value of heat pumps as flexible grid assets are key to maintaining momentum through 2030 and beyond.
